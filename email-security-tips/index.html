Email Security Tips

Your email account is the gateway to your entire digital life. Password resets, banking alerts, two-factor codes — they all flow through your inbox. If someone gains access to it, they can work their way into almost everything else.

The good news is that a handful of practical steps can dramatically reduce your risk. None of this requires deep technical knowledge — just a bit of awareness and the willingness to make a few changes.

Essentials

  • Use a Custom Domain

    Owning your own domain (e.g. you@yourdomain.com) means you're never locked into a single provider. If your current service shuts down or you want to switch, you just update your DNS records — no need to change your address everywhere.

  • Use Aliases and Anonymous Forwarding

    Give each service a unique email address (e.g. service123@yourdomain.com). Then, when you start receiving spam, you'll know exactly which service leaked your email, and you can disable that alias with one-click. Services like AnonAddy and SimpleLogin make this easy.

  • Secure Your Account with a Strong Password and 2FA

    Use a long, strong, unique password. Enable 2FA (such as a YubiKey or authenticator app) separate from your password manager.

  • Keep Regular Backups

    Use an email client like Thunderbird to sync your mailbox over IMAP so you always have a local copy. This protects you against account lockouts, provider outages, or unexpected service shutdowns.

Watch Out for Info Leakage

  • Don't Click Unsubscribe in Unsolicited Mail

    If you receive spam or an email you never signed up for, don't hit "unsubscribe." Doing so only confirms that your email address is active and in use. Mark it as spam instead.

  • Be Careful with Auto-Replies

    Out-of-office replies are handy, but they can reveal more than you intend: your info, travel dates, other contacts, and the fact that you're not monitoring your inbox. Ensure auto replies are only sent to known contacts, and avoid including personal info.

  • Watch Your Email Signature

    You don't know how secure your recipient's environment is. For example, there are browser extensions (like ZoomInfo), which can automatically scrape email signatures and build contact databases

Be Vigilant of Incoming Mail

  • Don't Click Links Without Checking

    Phishing emails are getting more convincing. Before clicking any link, hover over it to see where it actually leads. If the domain doesn't match the expected sender, don't click it. When in doubt, go directly to the website by typing the address into your browser.

  • Verify the Sender's Domain

    Check that the "From" address actually matches who the email claims to be from. Spoofed emails often come from domains that look similar but aren't quite right (e.g. paypa1.com instead of paypal.com). If your provider supports it, check the SPF/DKIM/DMARC headers for additional verification.

  • Be Cautious with Attachments

    Malicious files can be easily disguised, especially when sent in a compressed/ZIP file.

  • Don't connect third-party apps to your inbox

    Services like Unroll.me or SaneBox get full access to all your email content, and commonly upload this to their servers for processing.

Encryption

  • Use Encryption for Sensitive Data

    Without encryption, email is plaintext. Your provider, the recipient's provider, and anyone in between can read the content. Encryption ensures only you and the intended recipient can see the message

  • Choose an Encrypted Provider

    Where possible use email services which support end-to-end encryption, or encrypt your messages via PGP. Remember, your messages are only encrypted end-to-end when both the sender and the recipient have encryption set up. TLS alone is not enough

Audit Your Account Regularly

  • Check Your Forwarding Rules

    A common tactic for attackers is to set up a forwarding rule, to silently send all your incoming mail to an address they control. Periodically check your email settings, forwarding rules, filters and auto-replies for any changes you didn't make.

  • Review Connected Sessions

    Most email providers let you see a list of active sessions and recent login activity. If you see any logins you don't recognize, revoke it immediately and change your password.

  • Verify Recovery Settings

    Make sure that your account recovery email/phone are still yours and up-to-date. An attacker who controls the recovery method can reset your password and gain access to your account. Likewise, without any recovery options configured, you will not be able to regain entry into your email if you forget/lose any of your credentials

Configure Your Email Settings

  • Disable Remote Content Loading

    HTML emails can load images and stylesheets from external servers. This tells the sender your IP address, device info, and the exact time you opened the message — a common tracking technique. Most email clients have an option to block remote content by default. See this article for more detail.

  • Disable Read Receipts

    Read receipts confirm to the sender that you opened their message and when. Turn them off or set to "ask before sending" in your email client settings.

  • Turn Off Auto-Download of Attachments

    Some clients auto-download or preview attachments. Disable this so nothing runs without your explicit approval.

  • Hide Email Previews on Your Lock Screen

    Notifications can show sender, subject, and a snippet to anyone nearby. Set notifications to hide content when your device is locked.

  • Use Plaintext

    Most privacy and security threats with email (like tracking pixels, disguised links, and parser exploits) come from loading of dynamic HTML content. For optimal protection, you can set your client to compose and display plaintext.Although plaintext is no longer widely supported, and so can make mail harder to read.

Choose a Good Email Provider

  • Avoid Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo, and GMX

    Major free email providers make money from your data. Gmail has been caught giving third parties access to user emails and tracking purchases. Yahoo scanned emails in real time for government agencies. If email privacy matters to you, these are not good choices.

  • Think Twice Before Self-Hosting

    Running your own mail server gives you total control, but correctly configuring and securing one is genuinely difficult. Misconfiguration can lead to your server being used for spam or your messages being rejected by other providers. For most people, a trustworthy hosted provider is a better trade-off. If you do want to try, Mail-in-a-Box and docker-mailserver are solid starting points.

  • Understand that you might need to pay

    Typically, if a service is free - then you are the product. A paid email service has a business model that doesn't depend on mining your data. Likewise, owning a domain will cost a few dollars a year, but is worth it to retain control of your address

What to Look for in a Provider

  • Jurisdiction

    Where your provider is incorporated matters. Countries in the Five Eyes alliance (US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand) have broad surveillance powers and intelligence-sharing agreements. Providers based in privacy-friendly jurisdictions like Switzerland, Iceland, or Germany offer stronger legal protections for your data.

  • Encryption Support

    Look for a provider that supports end-to-end encryption — ideally built into the service so it works automatically.

  • Open Source

    Providers whose code is open source allow independent security researchers to audit it. This transparency makes it much harder to hide backdoors or poor security practices.

  • Custom Domain Support

    A good provider should let you use your own domain. This ensures portability — you can switch providers without changing your email address.

  • IMAP Support

    IMAP access lets you use third-party clients and keep local backups of your mailbox. Providers that only offer a web interface lock you into their platform.

  • Independent Security Audit

    Has the provider been audited by an independent third party? A published audit report demonstrates that the provider is willing to have its security claims verified.

  • Actively Maintained

    Check when the provider last released an update or responded to a security issue. A service that hasn't been updated in years may have unpatched vulnerabilities. An actively maintained product is more likely to respond quickly to new threats.

  • Anonymous Sign-Up and Payment

    If anonymity is important to you, look for providers that don't require personal information to create an account and accept anonymous payment methods like cryptocurrency or cash.

  • Onion Site / Tor Access

    For ultimate anonymity, some providers offer an .onion address. This allows high-value users to access their mail over the Tor network, which provides an extra layer of anonymity by hiding your IP address fully
